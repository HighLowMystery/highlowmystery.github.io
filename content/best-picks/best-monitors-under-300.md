## Why Your Monitor Matters More Than You Think

Two things happen when your monitor is wrong:

**Too low:** You tilt your head down to see the screen. Over eight hours, that's your neck under sustained load. Six months of that is how you end up with a physio bill. See the [neck pain working from home guide](/how-to/how-to-reduce-neck-pain-working-from-home/) for the full fix.

**Too small:** You lean forward to read. That's compressive load on your lumbar spine every time you check your email.

The fix is a 24-27 inch monitor at eye level — top of screen at or just below your eyes, arm's length away. Add a monitor arm ($25-35) to position it correctly. That's it.

---

## What Matters in a Home Office Monitor

**Panel type:** IPS or VA. Not TN. IPS gives you good color and wide viewing angles — important for color accuracy and for when someone walks behind you and needs to see your screen.

**Resolution:** 1080p is fine for 24 inch. For 27 inch, go 1440p if budget allows — 1080p at 27 inches starts to look soft up close.

**Refresh rate:** 60Hz is perfectly fine for office work. Don't pay extra for 144Hz unless you game.

**Connectivity:** HDMI is standard. USB-C with power delivery (65W+) is a serious quality-of-life upgrade — one cable from your laptop handles video + charging. Worth the price difference if your laptop supports it. If you need more ports, see the [best USB-C hubs](/best-picks/best-usb-c-hub-home-office/).

**Stand adjustability:** Height adjustment is minimum. Tilt + swivel is nice. Most cheap stands are fixed height — buy a monitor arm and skip the stand entirely. If you're on a laptop, a [laptop stand](/best-picks/best-laptop-stand-home-office/) alongside a monitor is the best dual-display setup.

## Monitor Setup: Getting It Right

**Height:** Top of screen should be at or just below eye level. If your monitor is too low, add a monitor arm or stack books under it temporarily to test.

**Distance:** Arm's length. Reach out — your fingertips should just touch the screen. Closer = eye strain. Farther = neck strain from leaning in.

**Angle:** Tilt it back slightly so you're looking at the screen straight on rather than down at an angle.

**Lighting:** No bright window behind the monitor (screen glare). Ideally, the window is to the side or you're facing it.

{{< callout type="tip" >}}
**Quick test:** After an hour of work, where does your neck feel tight? Upper neck = screen too low. Lower neck/upper back = screen too far. Neither = you nailed the position.
