Why Your Monitor Matters More Than You Think

Two things happen when your monitor is wrong:

Too low: You tilt your head down to see the screen. Over eight hours, that’s your neck under sustained load. Six months of that is how you end up with a physio bill.

Too small: You lean forward to read. That’s compressive load on your lumbar spine every time you check your email.

The fix is a 24-27 inch monitor at eye level — top of screen at or just below your eyes, arm’s length away. Add a monitor arm ($25-35) to position it correctly. That’s it.

What Matters in a Home Office Monitor

Panel type: IPS or VA. Not TN. IPS gives you good color and wide viewing angles — important for color accuracy and for when someone walks behind you and needs to see your screen.

Resolution: 1080p is fine for 24 inch. For 27 inch, go 1440p if budget allows — 1080p at 27 inches starts to look soft up close.

Refresh rate: 60Hz is perfectly fine for office work. Don’t pay extra for 144Hz unless you game.

Connectivity: HDMI is standard. USB-C with power delivery (65W+) is a serious quality-of-life upgrade — one cable from your laptop handles video + charging. Worth the price difference if your laptop supports it.

Stand adjustability: Height adjustment is minimum. Tilt + swivel is nice. Most cheap stands are fixed height — buy a monitor arm and skip the stand entirely.

Monitor Setup: Getting It Right

Height: Top of screen should be at or just below eye level. If your monitor is too low, add a monitor arm or stack books under it temporarily to test.

Distance: Arm’s length. Reach out — your fingertips should just touch the screen. Closer = eye strain. Farther = neck strain from leaning in.

Angle: Tilt it back slightly so you’re looking at the screen straight on rather than down at an angle.

Lighting: No bright window behind the monitor (screen glare). Ideally, the window is to the side or you’re facing it.

💡
Quick test: After an hour of work, where does your neck feel tight? Upper neck = screen too low. Lower neck/upper back = screen too far. Neither = you nailed the position.

FAQ

Frequently Asked Questions

24 inch vs 27 inch — which should I get?

27 inch if your desk has the depth (at least 24 inches from screen to eyes). 24 inch for smaller desks or tighter spaces. For side-by-side dual monitors, 24 inches each is often better than two 27s.

Do I need 4K for office work?

At 27 inches, 1440p is the sweet spot. 4K is beautiful but requires more GPU power and costs significantly more. For word processing and spreadsheets, 1440p is effectively the same at normal viewing distance.

IPS vs VA vs TN — what's the practical difference?

IPS: good color, good viewing angles, moderate contrast. VA: excellent contrast (great for dark content), average color, viewing angles are okay. TN: fast response, poor color, bad viewing angles — fine for gaming, not ideal for remote work. Buy IPS for a home office.

Is a curved monitor worth it for remote work?

For a single ultrawide (34+ inches), yes — the curve reduces eye travel. For a standard 27-inch, the curve is mostly aesthetic. I wouldn't pay extra for it. ---
